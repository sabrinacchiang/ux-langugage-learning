# Problem Definition

## Background

Adult language learners start and stop language learning repeatedly.

They download an app, complete lessons for a few days or weeks, lose momentum, stop practicing, and eventually return months later when a new motivation emerges.

This pattern is often treated as a motivation problem.

Research suggests otherwise.

Many adult learners have strong reasons for learning a language: relationships, family, travel, identity, culture, career growth, or a desire to connect more deeply with the world around them.

The issue is not desire.

The issue is that existing tools fail to support the psychological conditions required for language acquisition and long-term engagement.

---

## Key Research Findings

### Emotional Safety

Emotional Safety is derived from Krashen's Affective Filter Hypothesis.

Language acquisition is influenced by a learner's emotional state. When learners feel anxious, embarrassed, judged, or pressured, their ability to process and retain language decreases.

Emotional safety exists when learners feel free to attempt the language without fear of consequences.

Common violations in existing tools include:

* Streak loss mechanics
* Punishment for missed practice
* Heart systems that block progression after mistakes
* Competitive leaderboards
* Immediate performance pressure
* Speaking requirements before learners feel ready

When emotional safety is absent, learners disengage during practice.

---

### Identity Proximity

Identity Proximity is derived from Dörnyei's L2 Motivational Self System.

Long-term motivation depends on a learner's relationship to their future self as a speaker of the language.

When learners can clearly imagine who they are becoming, motivation remains strong.

When that future identity becomes blurry or feels unattainable, motivation declines.

Factors that weaken identity proximity include:

* Progress systems focused on XP or lesson completion
* Generic content disconnected from the learner's life
* Lack of visible real-world capability growth
* No reflection on personal progress
* Experiences that reinforce how far away fluency feels

When identity proximity fades, learners stop returning.

---

## The Episodic Learner

Research identified a common learner pattern: the Episodic Learner.

An episodic learner does not quit permanently.

Instead, they repeatedly start, stop, and restart language learning over time.

This learner experiences two primary failure moments:

### Failure Moment 1: During Practice

Emotional safety breaks down.

The learner feels overwhelmed, judged, pressured, or incapable.

They disengage from the session.

### Failure Moment 2: Between Sessions

Identity proximity fades.

Progress feels invisible.

The future self they were working toward becomes difficult to imagine.

They do not return.

---

## Problem Statement

Adult language learners who return to learning repeatedly are not failing from lack of desire.

They are caught in a predictable cycle:

Friction slows visible progress.

↓

Stagnation widens the gap between where they are and who they want to become.

↓

Their identity as a future speaker weakens.

↓

Motivation collapses.

↓

They stop learning.

Existing language learning tools focus on engagement metrics, lesson completion, and habit formation rather than the human conditions required for language acquisition.

As a result, learners disengage not because they lack motivation, but because the experience fails to maintain emotional safety and a believable sense of becoming a speaker.

---

## Design Opportunity

How might we bring the two conditions episodic adult learners are most missing — emotional safety to attempt the language without fear, and a sustained sense of becoming a speaker — into the fragmented, returnable moments of everyday life?

---

## Design Principles

### Design for Real Adult Lives

Learning must fit into fragmented moments throughout the day.

Examples:

* Waiting in line
* Commuting
* During a lunch break
* Before bed

### Design for Return

Absence should be treated as normal behavior, not failure.

The experience should make returning feel natural, regardless of how much time has passed.

### Design for Meaningful Context

Language should be connected to situations, people, and experiences that matter to the learner.

Context creates both emotional safety and identity proximity.

### Design for Becoming

Progress should reinforce who the learner is becoming rather than what they have completed.

Capability matters more than activity.

Identity matters more than streaks.
